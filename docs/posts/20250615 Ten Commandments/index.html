The dates of Easter and Passover vary each year because both are tied to lunisolar calendars, but they follow different systems. Here‚Äôs a breakdown of how each is determined:

Easter is celebrated on:

The first Sunday after the first full moon on or after the vernal equinox
(fixed as March 21 in church calculations)

Step-by-step:

  1. Identify March 21 (ecclesiastical spring equinox).
  2. Find the first full moon (called the Paschal Full Moon) after March 21.
  3. Easter is the following Sunday.
  • This is based on the Gregorian calendar (used in Western Christianity).

Eastern Orthodox Easter uses the Julian calendar, which often results in a different date.

‚ú°Ô∏è Passover Date Determination

Passover begins on:

The 15th day of the Hebrew month of Nisan, which is based on the Jewish lunisolar calendar.

Details:

  1. Nisan begins with the new moon closest to the spring equinox.
  2. Passover always begins at sundown on 15 Nisan.
  3. It often falls on or near the full moon, just like Easter, but is based on the Hebrew calendar, not the Gregorian one.

Duration:

  • 7 days in Israel
  • 8 days in the diaspora (for traditional Jews)

Easter often falls during or shortly after Passover, because the crucifixion of Jesus is described as occurring during Passover.

However, due to different calendar systems and adjustments (e.g., leap months in the Jewish calendar), the two don‚Äôt always align.

üîÅ Patterns and Exceptions

The Jewish calendar occasionally adds a leap month (Adar II) to stay in sync with the solar year. This can shift Passover later, causing Easter to occur before Passover in rare years.

The Council of Nicaea (325 AD) fixed the method for calculating Easter, decoupling it from the Jewish calendar, which is why the dates are similar but not identical.
